The user wants to paint a watercolor version of the image provided.

Analyse the image carefully and produce a painting guide structured into the four sections below. Each section should stand alone — a painter jumping straight to "Step-by-Step" should have everything they need without having to re-read earlier sections. Be specific to what you actually see in the image — avoid generic advice that could apply to any painting.

---

## Overview

Give the painter a fast mental map of the whole painting before they start.

- Describe the scene, its mood, and the overall atmosphere to capture.
- Summarise the **light source** (direction, quality — hard/soft, warm/cool).
- Identify the **focal point** and note which areas are supporting cast.
- Break the image into **3–5 tonal zones** (lightest light → darkest dark) and say which parts of the scene fall into each.
- Call out any areas to **reserve as white paper** — these must be masked or carefully avoided from the very start.
- Recommend **paper weight and texture** (e.g. 300gsm cold press for soft edges, hot press for detail), whether to stretch it, and any key brushes or masking fluid needed.
- Close with a one-sentence **Painting Order Summary** — the sequence from first wash to final mark — so the painter can keep it as a quick reference while working.

---

## Composition

Help the painter make deliberate decisions about the layout before pencil hits paper.

- Recommend paper **orientation** (portrait/landscape) and a suggested **aspect ratio or crop** if the composition would benefit from one.
- Identify the **focal point placement** (e.g. rule of thirds) and explain the reasoning.
- Note **leading lines, framing elements, or shapes** that guide the eye toward the focal point.
- Identify any elements to **simplify or omit** that would compete with the focal point or add clutter.
- Describe any **negative space** that should be preserved, and how much of the paper it should occupy.
- Note **foreground / mid-ground / background** separation and how to differentiate them (value, edge quality, detail level).

---

## Palette & Colour

Give the painter a complete, specific colour plan to mix from before starting.

- Suggest a **limited palette of 4–7 pigments** suited to the scene. For each, name the pigment or a well-known equivalent (e.g. "Burnt Sienna — PBr7") and its role in the painting.
- Identify the **dominant temperature** (warm or cool) and any temperature contrasts to exploit.
- If there is a strong atmospheric colour (e.g. golden hour, overcast grey, forest green ambient), describe how to bias the whole palette toward it.
- List any **key mixes** to prepare in advance (colours, approximate ratios, intended use).
- Flag any pigments to **avoid mixing together** (muddying combinations specific to this palette).
- Note which pigments are **transparent and non-staining** (suited to glazing) vs opaque or granulating (use with care in later layers).

---

## Step-by-Step

Walk the painter through every stage in application order. Number each step. Be specific about colours, timing, and technique.

1. **Masking** — if any whites are too intricate to paint around, apply masking fluid now and let it dry fully.
2. **Underpainting / Ground Wash** — describe the overall first wash: colour(s), coverage, whether to wet the paper first, and which areas to leave dry. Explain how it unifies the painting and establishes the dominant temperature.
3. **Wet-in-Wet Passages** — identify areas best handled wet-in-wet (skies, backgrounds, reflections, soft foliage). For each: timing (how wet the surface should be), colours to drop in, degree of control needed, and any traps to watch for (blooms, muddy mixes).
4. **First Wet-on-Dry Layers** — once earlier washes are fully dry, describe the second-pass glazes to build form and shadow. Note which edges should be hard (wet-on-dry) vs softened (lifted or blended while damp). Include any negative painting opportunities.
5. **Glazing & Colour Depth** — recommend any transparent glaze passes to deepen shadows or shift colour temperature. Name the specific pigments suited to glazing and which to avoid.
6. **Texture Techniques** — based on what is in the image, suggest any of the following where appropriate and say exactly where to apply them:
   - **Salt** (granular texture — rough stone, sand, foliage)
   - **Dry brush** (broken edges — grass, water sparkle, rough wood)
   - **Lifting** (clouds, light on water, fur highlights)
   - **Spattering** (gravel, foliage texture, snow)
   - **Scraping / sgraffito** (fine light lines — grasses, rigging, hair)
   - **Plastic wrap / cling film** (random organic texture — rocks, ice, bark)
7. **Foreground & Final Details** — describe the darkest darks to add last, placed deliberately near the focal point. Note any calligraphic marks or fine detail (e.g. branches, window frames, figure outlines) and the brush/technique to use. Suggest any final colour accents or complementary touches.
8. **What to Avoid** — call out 2–4 specific pitfalls this particular image presents (e.g. overworking a complex reflection, losing sky luminosity with too many layers, muddy mixing of specific colour combinations).
