description: >
  Accepts an image and produces a step-by-step guide for painting a watercolor
  version of it — covering composition, value mapping, colour palette, and each
  standard watercolor technique in the order they would be applied (wet-in-wet,
  wet-on-dry, glazing, etc.). Use when a user shares a photo or image and asks
  how to paint it in watercolor, or wants watercolor painting guidance.
argument-hint: "[attach or describe an image]"

---

The user wants to paint a watercolor version of the image provided.

Analyse the image carefully and produce a structured painting guide in the sections below, in order. Be specific to what you actually see in the image — avoid generic advice that could apply to any painting.

---

## 1. First Impressions & Composition

- Describe the overall scene and its mood/atmosphere.
- Identify the **focal point** and explain where on the paper it should sit (e.g. rule of thirds placement).
- Note any leading lines, framing elements, or shapes that guide the eye.
- Recommend paper **orientation** (portrait/landscape) and a suggested **aspect ratio** or crop if the composition would benefit from one.
- Suggest whether to preserve the composition as-is or simplify/omit any elements that would fight the focal point.

## 2. Value Map & Tonal Plan

- Describe the **light source** direction and quality (hard/soft, warm/cool).
- Break the image into **3–5 tonal zones** (lightest light → darkest dark) and identify which parts of the scene fall into each zone.
- Call out any areas to **reserve as white paper** — these must be masked or carefully painted around from the very start.
- Note any dramatic contrast areas that will anchor the composition.

## 3. Colour Palette

- Suggest a **limited palette** of 4–7 pigments suited to the scene. For each colour name the pigment or a well-known equivalent (e.g. "Burnt Sienna — PBr7").
- Identify the **dominant temperature** (warm or cool) and any temperature contrasts to exploit.
- Note any colours to **mix** rather than use straight from the tube.
- If there is a strong atmospheric colour (e.g. golden hour, overcast grey, forest green ambient light), describe how to bias the whole palette toward it.

## 4. Paper & Materials

- Recommend **paper weight and texture** (e.g. 300gsm cold press for soft edges, hot press for detail).
- Suggest whether to **pre-wet and stretch** the paper.
- Note any brushes particularly suited to this subject (e.g. large round mop for skies, rigger for fine branches).
- Mention masking fluid if white areas are intricate.

## 5. Underpainting / Initial Wash

- Describe the **overall ground wash** to lay first — colour(s), coverage, and whether to wet the paper first.
- Explain how this wash unifies the painting and establishes the dominant temperature.
- Note areas to leave dry or partially dry before proceeding.

## 6. Wet-in-Wet Passages

- Identify which areas of the image are best handled **wet-in-wet** (soft, diffused edges — typically skies, backgrounds, reflections, fog, soft foliage masses).
- For each passage: describe the timing (how wet the surface should be), the colours to drop in, and the degree of control needed.
- Warn about any wet-in-wet traps specific to this image (e.g. blooms, colours turning muddy if overmixed).

## 7. Wet-on-Dry Layers (Mid-ground Detail)

- Once earlier washes are fully dry, describe the **second and third glaze layers** to build form and shadow.
- Identify which edges should be **hard** (wet-on-dry) vs **softened** (lifted or blended while damp).
- Note any **negative painting** opportunities — painting the background shape to reveal a lighter foreground form.

## 8. Glazing & Colour Depth

- Recommend any **transparent glaze passes** needed to deepen shadows or shift colour temperature (e.g. a violet glaze over warm shadow areas).
- Name the specific pigments suited to glazing (transparent, non-staining) vs those to avoid (opaque, granulating pigments sit on top rather than glazing).

## 9. Texture Techniques

- Based on what is in the image, suggest any of the following where appropriate, being specific about where to apply them:
  - **Salt** (granular texture — rough stone, sand, foliage)
  - **Dry brush** (broken edges — grass, water sparkle, rough wood)
  - **Lifting** (clouds, light on water, fur highlights)
  - **Spattering** (gravel, foliage texture, snow)
  - **Scraping / sgrafitto** (fine light lines — grasses, rigging, hair)
  - **Plastic wrap / cling film** (random organic texture — rocks, ice, bark)

## 10. Foreground & Final Details

- Describe the **darkest darks** to add last — these will make the lights sing, so place them deliberately near the focal point.
- Note any **calligraphic marks or fine detail** (e.g. branches, window frames, figure outlines) and the brush/technique to use.
- Suggest any final **colour accents** or complementary touches to add energy.

## 11. What to Avoid

- Call out 2–4 specific pitfalls this particular image presents for a watercolorist (e.g. overworking a complex reflection, losing the luminosity of a sky by adding too many layers, muddy mixing of specific colour combinations in the palette).

---

End with a one-paragraph **Painting Order Summary** that recaps the recommended sequence from first wash to final mark, so the painter can keep it as a quick reference while working.
