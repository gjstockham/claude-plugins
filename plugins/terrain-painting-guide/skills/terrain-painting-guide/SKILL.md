The user wants to paint a tabletop terrain piece. They may have shared a photo of the unpainted piece, or described it in text (e.g. "a ruined stone tower with a wooden door and mossy base").

Analyse what you can see or what has been described, and produce a painting guide structured into the four sections below. Each section should stand alone — a painter jumping straight to "Step-by-Step" should have everything they need without re-reading earlier sections. Be specific to the actual piece — avoid generic advice that could apply to any terrain.

If working from a description rather than an image, make reasonable assumptions about the materials (resin, foam, plaster, MDF) and ask the user to clarify if a key detail would significantly change the advice.

---

## Overview

Give the painter a fast mental map of the whole piece before they start.

- Identify the **terrain type** (dungeon stone, natural rock, wood, ruins, earth, snow, desert, etc.) and the **overall feel** to aim for — grim and weathered, vibrant fantasy, realistic, etc.
- Identify the **primary material** the piece is made from if visible or stated (resin, foam/XPS, plaster/Hydrostone, MDF, plastic, air-dry clay, natural materials). Note any implications for priming or paint adhesion.
- Break the piece into **2–4 material zones** (e.g. stone walls, wooden beams, dirt base, metal fittings) and name the dominant colour family for each.
- Identify the **focal point** of the piece (the most visually interesting area — an archway, a door, a crumbling parapet) and note which areas are secondary.
- Summarise the **light assumption** — terrain is typically lit from above, so raised surfaces get highlighted and recesses get darkened. Note any exceptions (glowing runes, lanterns, lava).
- Recommend any **materials or tools** specific to this piece:
  - Primer type (spray vs brush-on; black, grey, or brown undercoat)
  - Speciality products (texture paste, crackle medium, water effects, static grass, tufts, flock, sand, ballast)
  - Brush types (large flat for drybrushing, old stiff brush for stippling, fine detail brush)
- Close with a one-sentence **Painting Order Summary** — the sequence from primer to final scenic details.

---

## Colour Plan

Help the painter decide the overall colour story before mixing anything.

- For each **material zone** identified in Overview, give a target colour direction with a brief rationale (e.g. "stone walls: cool blue-grey, slightly greenish where damp and mossy").
- Identify the **dominant colour temperature** (warm torchlit dungeon, cool moonlit ruins, earthy natural) and how to keep all zones harmonious within it.
- Note any **colour accents** to introduce for visual interest — a pop of colour on a painted door, oxidised copper on metal fittings, orange lichen in a crack.
- Describe the **weathering character** appropriate to this piece: how dirty, how worn, how overgrown. This should inform how heavily to apply washes and how much to drybrush.
- If the base/groundwork is part of the piece, describe the **base colour story** separately and explain how it should frame or complement the main piece.

---

## Palette & Colours

Give a complete colour list to have ready before starting. Use generic colour descriptions — the painter will match these from their own acrylic collection (craft, artist, or metallic acrylics all work fine for terrain).

Organise by zone. For each colour, describe its role and approximate hue clearly.

- **Base coat colours** — the main flat colour that goes down after priming. Describe as a specific hue and value (e.g. "mid grey-brown", "dark charcoal grey", "warm tan").
- **Wash colours** — a heavily diluted dark colour (black, brown, dark green, etc.) thinned with water to a very fluid consistency and flooded into recesses. Describe the appropriate wash tone per zone (e.g. "very dilute black-brown for stone", "very dilute dark brown for wood", "very dilute dark green for vegetation areas").
- **Drybrush colours** — typically 2–3 progressively lighter tones for each material zone, applied nearly dry to catch raised edges. Name each tone (e.g. "mid grey → light grey → off-white" for stone).
- **Detail colours** — for specific small elements. Be descriptive (e.g. "dark rusty orange-brown for iron fittings", "bright yellow-green for lichen", "cream or pale yellow for candle wax").
- **Basing & scenic materials** — list what the painter will need (PVA glue, fine sand or ballast, flock, tufts, static grass, water effects gel or gloss varnish for puddles). These are materials, not paints — describe colours where relevant (e.g. "mid brown for the ground, dry-brushed sandy beige").
- **Varnish** — matte for most of the piece; gloss or satin for any water, wet stone, or glass elements.

Flag which colours are **essential** to the look and which are **optional enhancements**.

## Step-by-Step

Walk the painter through every stage in the correct order. Number each step. Be specific about colours, techniques, and timing. Describe colours generically (e.g. "dark grey-brown", "light stone grey") rather than brand names — the painter is working from their own acrylic collection.

1. **Preparation** — describe any prep needed before priming: filling gaps (Polyfilla/spackle or PVA + baking soda), sealing foam if present (PVA wash or foam sealant — spray primer WILL melt bare XPS/polystyrene foam unless it is sealed or the primer is water-based), adding pre-base texture (sand, ballast, gravel glued with PVA), and letting everything dry fully.

2. **Priming** — recommend a specific undercoat colour and application method for this piece. Explain why that colour (e.g. black primer for dark stone so recesses are pre-shaded; grey for a neutral mid-tone start; brown for warm earthy terrain). Note spray distance and coverage tips.

3. **Base Coating** — describe the first opaque colour pass across each zone. Note whether to use thinned paint (2:1 paint:water for smooth coverage) or undiluted, and whether a large flat brush or an airbrush would help. The goal is solid, even coverage — not perfection.

4. **Washing / Shading** — describe the wash pass(es): which product goes where, how to apply it (flood the whole surface vs targeted recess-lining), and how long to let it dry before the next step. Note any areas to avoid over-washing (very light surfaces can go muddy). Recommend a second targeted wash in deep recesses if needed.

5. **Drybrushing** — this is the primary highlighting technique for terrain. Describe 2–3 drybrush passes per major zone, each with a progressively lighter colour. Explain how dry the brush should be (wipe on tissue until almost no paint transfers, then apply with a quick scrubbing motion across raised surfaces). Identify which direction to drybrush (upward strokes on vertical walls, circular on flat ground).

6. **Detail Painting** — describe the small accent areas in order of what to paint first: doors and wood grain, metal fittings, vegetation (moss, lichen, vines), candles or lanterns, runes or symbols, water surfaces. For each, name the colours and any technique (stippling for rough moss, thin line work for runes).

7. **Basing & Groundwork** — if the piece has an integrated base, describe how to finish it: applying texture paste or PVA + sand, painting the ground, adding flock or tufts. Specify glue-down technique (diluted PVA 1:1 with water), placement of different materials (coarser gravel to edges, finer flock near the terrain itself), and sealing with thinned PVA or matte varnish.

8. **Scenic Effects** — if applicable to this piece, describe any special effects in the correct order:
   - **Water / puddles**: apply after all other painting; pour in thin layers, let each cure fully
   - **Snow**: apply last, over sealed base; PVA + baking soda mix or dedicated snow flock
   - **OSL (Object Source Lighting)**: thin, brightly coloured glaze radiating outward from light sources
   - **Crackle / dried mud**: apply thickly and let dry without disturbing
   - **Rust / verdigris**: stippled orange-brown over metal then a thin green wash in recesses

9. **Varnishing** — apply matte varnish across the whole piece to unify sheen and protect the paint. Note any areas to avoid (or re-gloss after) — standing water, wet stone, gem stones, candle flames.

10. **What to Avoid** — call out 2–4 specific pitfalls this particular piece presents (e.g. melting foam with solvent-based spray primer, over-drybrushing and losing the shadow in recesses, applying static grass before varnishing so it gets flattened, adding flock before the wash is fully dry).
