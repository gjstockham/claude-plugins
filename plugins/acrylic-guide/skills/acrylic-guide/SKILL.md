The user wants to paint an acrylic version of the image provided.

Analyse the image carefully and produce a painting guide structured into the four sections below. Each section should stand alone — a painter jumping straight to "Step-by-Step" should have everything they need without having to re-read earlier sections. Be specific to what you actually see in the image — avoid generic advice that could apply to any painting.

---

## Overview

Give the painter a fast mental map of the whole painting before they start.

- Describe the scene, its mood, and the overall atmosphere to capture.
- Summarise the **light source** (direction, quality — hard/soft, warm/cool).
- Identify the **focal point** and note which areas are supporting cast.
- Break the image into **3–5 tonal zones** (lightest light → darkest dark) and say which parts of the scene fall into each.
- Note the **overall value structure** — acrylics can go light over dark, so flag where you'll build up to highlights rather than reserve them.
- Recommend **surface and ground** (e.g. canvas board vs stretched canvas, linen, gessoed wood panel; whether a toned ground would help establish the mid-tone quickly).
- Call out any key **mediums** that will matter for this image (retarder for wet blending, glazing medium for transparent layers, impasto medium for texture, open medium to extend working time).
- Close with a one-sentence **Painting Order Summary** — the sequence from ground layer to final marks — so the painter can keep it as a quick reference while working.

---

## Composition

Help the painter make deliberate decisions about the layout before anything goes on the surface.

- Recommend **orientation** (portrait/landscape) and a suggested **aspect ratio or crop** if the composition would benefit from one.
- Identify the **focal point placement** (e.g. rule of thirds) and explain the reasoning.
- Note **leading lines, framing elements, or shapes** that guide the eye toward the focal point.
- Identify any elements to **simplify or omit** that would compete with the focal point or add clutter.
- Describe any **negative space** that should be preserved, and how much of the surface it should occupy.
- Note **foreground / mid-ground / background** separation and how to differentiate them (value, edge quality, detail level, colour temperature).

---

## Palette & Colour

Give the painter a complete, specific colour plan to mix before starting.

- Suggest a **limited palette of 4–7 pigments** suited to the scene. For each, name the pigment or a well-known equivalent (e.g. "Burnt Sienna — PBr7"), its role, and whether to use it neat or heavily diluted.
- Identify the **dominant temperature** (warm or cool) and any temperature contrasts to exploit.
- If there is a strong atmospheric colour (e.g. golden hour, overcast grey, forest green ambient), describe how to bias the whole palette toward it.
- List any **key mixes** to prepare in advance (colours, approximate ratios, intended use).
- Flag **colours that shift on drying** — acrylics typically dry 5–15% darker; note where this matters most and how to compensate.
- Note which pigments are **transparent** (suited to glazing layers) vs **opaque** (good for coverage or highlights painted light-over-dark).
- Warn about any pigments prone to **lifting when re-wetted** or difficult to layer over cleanly.

---

## Step-by-Step

Walk the painter through every stage in application order. Number each step. Be specific about colours, mediums, timing, and technique. Flag at each wet-blending step that acrylics dry fast and a retarder or stay-wet palette is advisable.

1. **Toning the Ground** — describe whether to tone the surface and with what colour (a mid-tone or complementary colour relevant to this image). Explain how it unifies the painting and eliminates the glaring white that makes value judgements hard.
2. **Blocking In** — describe the first opaque or semi-opaque lay-in of the main shapes using thin paint. Identify the approximate colours, the areas to establish first, and how loosely to work at this stage. Note which edges matter and which can be ignored for now.
3. **Establishing Darks** — acrylics should generally go darks-first in opaque work (unlike glazing passes). Describe where to place the darkest darks, the specific colours to mix, and how to keep them rich rather than muddy. Note any areas where the toned ground can do this work.
4. **Building Mid-Tones & Form** — describe the main passes that model volume and surface. Note timing: each layer must be touch-dry (typically 10–30 min depending on thickness and environment) before painting over it. Identify where blending while wet is needed and suggest using a retarder medium or misting with water.
5. **Wet-Blending Passages** — identify areas that benefit from soft transitions (skies, skin, fog, reflections). For each: describe the colours involved, whether to work quickly or use a retarder, and how to soften edges (feathering with a dry brush, blending medium, misting).
6. **Glazing & Colour Depth** — recommend any transparent glaze passes (pigment + glazing medium, heavily diluted) to deepen shadows, shift colour temperature, or unify passages. Name the specific pigments suited to glazing and which opaques to avoid here.
7. **Texture Techniques** — based on what is in the image, suggest any of the following where appropriate and say exactly where to apply them:
   - **Impasto** (thick, sculptural marks — rough stone, heavy foliage, crashing water)
   - **Palette knife** (flat planes, hard edges, architectural surfaces, mixing smooth areas)
   - **Dry brush** (broken edges — grass, water sparkle, rough wood, fur)
   - **Spattering** (gravel, foliage texture, snow, spray)
   - **Sgraffito / scraping** (fine light lines into wet paint — grasses, rigging, hair)
   - **Stippling** (foliage clusters, rough stone, pointillist colour mixing)
   - **Pouring / flow techniques** (if suited to the image — atmospheric washes, water)
8. **Highlights & Final Details** — describe the lightest lights to add last, mixed opaquely and placed deliberately at the focal point. Note any calligraphic marks or fine detail and the brush/technique to use. Suggest any final colour accents or complementary touches.
9. **What to Avoid** — call out 2–4 specific pitfalls this particular image presents (e.g. overworking edges while paint is half-dry causing muddy drag marks, losing luminosity in glazes by adding white instead of using a glazing medium, colours looking wrong wet that shift noticeably on drying).
